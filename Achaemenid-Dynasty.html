Achaemenid Dynasty

The Achaemenid Empire (550–330 BCE), founded by Cyrus the Great, was the first Persian Empire and one of the largest in world history. It stretched from the Indus Valley to the Balkans and from the Caucasus to Egypt. The Achaemenids established a sophisticated administration based on satrapies (provinces) governed by regional satraps, introduced a uniform system of taxation, and built an advanced network of roads including the Royal Road. They encouraged freedom of religion and culture, pioneered early postal systems, and supported monumental architecture such as Persepolis and Susa. Notable figures include Cyrus the Great (founder and reformer of human rights), Darius I the Great (organizer and builder of Persepolis), and Xerxes I (leader of the Greco-Persian Wars). The dynasty’s fall to Alexander the Great in 330 BCE ended an era of Persian hegemony, but its legacy endured for millennia.
